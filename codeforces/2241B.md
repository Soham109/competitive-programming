# Good times Good times

> [Problem on Codeforces](https://codeforces.com/problemset/problem/2241/B)

## Idea
The key insight is to construct $y$ such that it is a power of $10$ plus $1$ (e.g., $11$, $101$, $1001$, etc.). This guarantees that $y$ is "good" because its decimal representation contains at most two distinct digits ($1$ and $0$). Furthermore, multiplying $x$ (which is already "good") by such a $y$ results in $x \times y$ being "good" because the multiplication only scales the digits of $x$ and appends zeros, preserving the distinct digit count.

## Approach
1. Observe that any "good" number $x$ has at most two distinct digits. Multiplying $x$ by a number $y$ that is a power of $10$ plus $1$ results in $x \times y$ being "good" because the multiplication does not introduce new digits.
2. To construct $y$, determine the smallest power of $10$ that is greater than $x$. This ensures that $x \times y$ will not introduce more than two distinct digits.
3. Add $1$ to this power of $10$ to form $y$. For example, if $x = 73$, the smallest power of $10$ greater than $x$ is $100$, so $y = 101$.
4. Output $y$ for each test case. This approach guarantees that both $y$ and $x \times y$ are "good" for any valid input $x$.

## Complexity
- **Time:** $O(\log x)$ per test case, where $\log x$ is the number of digits in $x$, because determining the smallest power of $10$ greater than $x$ requires iterating through the digits of $x$.
- **Space:** $O(1)$, as no additional storage is required beyond a few variables.

## Notes
The choice of $y$ as a power of $10$ plus $1$ is not unique but ensures correctness and simplicity. Other values of $y$ satisfying the conditions may also exist, but this choice is optimal for implementation simplicity.
