# Catching the Krug

> [Problem on Codeforces](https://codeforces.com/problemset/problem/2152/B)

## Idea

The key observation is that Doran's movement range is larger than the Krug's because Doran can also move diagonally. This means that Doran can always reduce the Manhattan distance to the Krug by at least $1$ on each of his turns, regardless of the Krug's movement. Therefore, the Krug's survival time is determined by the maximum possible Manhattan distance between their starting positions, which depends on the grid boundaries.

## Approach

1. Compute the Manhattan distance between the Krug's starting position $(r_K, c_K)$ and Doran's starting position $(r_D, c_D)$. The Manhattan distance is $\lvert r_K - r_D \rvert + \lvert c_K - c_D \rvert$.
2. Since Doran can reduce this distance by at least $1$ on each of his turns, the Krug's survival time is bounded by the maximum number of moves it takes for Doran to reach the Krug.
3. To maximize the survival time, the Krug will try to move toward the farthest corner of the grid from Doran's starting position. The four possible corners of the grid are $(0, 0)$, $(0, n)$, $(n, 0)$, and $(n, n)$.
4. For each corner, calculate the Manhattan distance between Doran's starting position and the corner. The Krug's survival time is the maximum of these distances because it represents the longest possible time Doran needs to reach the Krug if the Krug moves optimally.
5. Output the maximum distance as the Krug's survival time.

## Complexity

- **Time:** $O(1)$ per test case, as the solution involves a constant number of arithmetic operations to compute the Manhattan distances and find the maximum.
- **Space:** $O(1)$, since no additional memory is used beyond a fixed number of variables.

## Notes

- The solution leverages the fact that the Krug's movement is strictly less flexible than Doran's, making the Manhattan distance reduction a reliable measure of survival time.
- The grid size $n$ can be as large as $10^9$, but the solution avoids iterating over the grid by directly computing distances, ensuring efficiency.
